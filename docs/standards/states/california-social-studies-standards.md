# California Social Studies Standards

## Jurisdiction

- jurisdiction: `California`
- subject: `Social Studies`
- source profile: `California History-Social Science Content Standards and related framework surfaces`
- repository codification status: `Drafted`

## Public Source Surface

California publicly distributes a history-social science standards surface together with supporting framework materials.

For repository purposes, the important public surfaces are:

- history-social science content standards
- framework or implementation guidance materials
- grade-span and course-level organization cues

## Profile Summary

California uses a history-social science structure that combines elementary grade-level expectations with secondary course-oriented expectations. The state surface is broad enough to support inquiry, history, civics, geography, and economics work, but its public organizational grammar is not identical to the repository's shared-inquiry-first graph structure.

For repository purposes, California is best treated as a discipline-bundled state profile with explicit secondary course sensitivity.

## Relationship To Repository Social Studies Content Baseline

- relationship type: `discipline-bundled baseline-derived`
- core structure: `elementary grade-level expectations with secondary history-social science course organization`
- notable state distinction: `California's public organization is history-social-science centered rather than graph-first across inquiry, history, civics, geography, and economics`

## Relationship To Repository Performance Baseline

California's public social studies surface is primarily a content-organization taxonomy. Repository performance bands for explanation, evidence use, transfer, and public argument should remain separate unless California-specific performance-descriptor surfaces are later codified.

## Grade-Band Structure

### Kindergarten Through Grade 5

Primary organizational pattern:

- grade-specific history-social science expectations
- community, chronology, place, and civic-life themes
- early evidence and interpretation tasks embedded in content work

Operational fit with repository node work:

- strong fit for early inquiry, chronology, civic community, map and place, and introductory economic reasoning nodes

### Grades 6 Through 8

Primary organizational pattern:

- grade-level history-social science expectations
- expanding world-history, civics, geography, and economic-system content
- stronger document and source interpretation demands

Operational fit with repository node work:

- strong fit for intermediate history, civics, geography, and economics branches
- requires explicit crosswalk notes when one state expectation bundles multiple repository strands

### Grades 9 Through 12

Primary organizational pattern:

- course-oriented history-social science structure
- explicit treatment of government, economics, United States history, and world history contexts
- stronger analysis, argument, and document-based reasoning expectations

Operational fit with repository node work:

- strong fit for advanced civics, historical interpretation, economic systems, and public-argument nodes
- requires course-context metadata when a repository node is broader than a single California course

## Domain Or Strand Structure

California's public social studies surface is best understood through these repository-relevant strands:

- historical thinking and chronology
- civic institutions, participation, and constitutional government
- geography, regions, movement, and human-environment relationships
- economics, exchange, incentives, and systems
- culture, continuity, change, and public interpretation

The repository should also preserve any distinction California makes between standards content and supporting framework guidance.

## Notable Divergences Or State-Specific Terminology

- `History-Social Science` is the governing California term and should not be collapsed into generic `social studies`
- secondary expectations are often course-contextual rather than one continuous cross-grade graph
- framework guidance is important for interpretation but should not be confused with the standards identifiers themselves
