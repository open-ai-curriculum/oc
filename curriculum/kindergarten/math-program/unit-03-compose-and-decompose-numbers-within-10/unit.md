## Unit Purpose

- What is the unit about?
  - Students build numbers within 10 in more than one way using objects, drawings, ten-frames, and simple math stories.
- Why does it matter for learners?
  - Part-whole thinking is the bridge from counting to operations. Kindergarten students need repeated, concrete opportunities to make, break apart, and explain numbers before later addition and subtraction work.

## Desired Results

### Students should understand:

- a number can be made in different ways
- the same total can have different parts
- drawings, objects, and frames can all show the same quantity

### Students should know:

- numbers within 10 can be built and broken apart
- a missing part can be found with objects and discussion
- a math story can represent parts and a whole

### Students should be able to do:

- show a number in two ways
- build and break apart quantities within 10
- explain two ways to make the same number
- use objects, drawings, and frames to show parts and whole

## Essential Questions

- How can we make the same number in different ways?
- How do the parts and the whole go together?
- How can a picture or story show a number?

## Assessment Evidence

## Accessibility And Inclusion Notes

- use counters, part-whole mats, ten-frames, and teacher-recorded drawings
- allow pointing, moving objects, drawing, circling, and oral explanations
- keep the work concrete before asking for symbolic recording

## Subject-Specific Quality Notes

- What discipline-specific risks need attention?
  - treating composition as memorization instead of quantity relationships
  - moving to abstract symbols too early
  - confusing arrangement changes with quantity changes
- What content or task types require extra review?
  - part-whole representations
  - ten-frame tasks
  - missing-part prompts
