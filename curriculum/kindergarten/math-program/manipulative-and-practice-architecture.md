# Kindergarten Math Manipulative And Practice Architecture

## Purpose

Define the recurring student-material and teacher-material architecture for Kindergarten Math.

## Core Design Principles

- mathematics should stay concrete, visual, and discussable
- practice should not collapse into worksheet-only routines
- manipulatives, drawings, oral explanation, and teacher recording all count as core math evidence paths

## Student Material Types

### Daily Practice Pages

Use for:

- count-and-show routines
- compare-and-explain prompts
- draw-or-circle responses
- teacher-recorded math explanations

### Center Cards

Use for:

- sorting
- counting collections
- shape building
- matching representations
- quick review routines

### Math Journals Or Recording Frames

Use for:

- draw and label math stories
- circle how many
- show two ways to make a number
- record simple data

### Cut-And-Sort Pages

Use for:

- compare sets
- classify shapes
- match numeral, quantity, and picture

## Core Manipulatives

- counters
- connecting cubes
- ten-frames
- numeral cards
- shape tiles or cutouts
- counting mats
- measuring strips
- picture cards

## Teacher-Edition Components

Each future unit should provide:

- lesson opening routine
- teacher modeling moves
- questions for discussion
- likely misconceptions
- intervention pathway
- extension pathway
- multilingual and accessibility notes
- material preparation notes

## Family Support Assets

Math units should include:

- short family math notes
- home count-and-talk routines
- optional take-home practice pages

## Accessibility And Multilingual Considerations

- keep response paths open to pointing, moving objects, drawing, circling, and oral explanation
- use consistent visual models
- provide brief sentence frames for explanation
- avoid fine-motor output as the only valid evidence path
