## Alignment Notes

- This guide supports the unit goals around book handling, print awareness, and oral comprehension.
- It keeps the instructional focus on meaningful shared reading rather than isolated worksheet completion.
- The guide is intended to improve teacher usability without changing the literacy target or lowering the cognitive demand.

## Teacher Preparation Notes

- Choose a read-aloud text with strong illustrations, clear print placement, and enough interest for repeated discussion.
- Preview where you will pause to model front cover, title, author, and where reading begins.
- Prepare picture cards or gestures for key vocabulary so students can respond even with emerging oral language.

## Likely Misconceptions Or Developmental Challenges

- Students may confuse title, cover image, and story topic.
- Students may point randomly on a page rather than where print begins.
- Students may answer discussion prompts with prior experience instead of text-based details when prompts are too broad.
- Some students may understand book handling but need more language support to explain what they notice.

## Recommended Teacher Moves

- Model once, then invite students to imitate with gestures and pointing.
- Keep prompts concrete: “Show me where I start reading” is stronger than “What do readers do?”
- Revoice student responses in complete sentences without shutting down emerging language.
- Use pictures and repeated phrases to keep the discussion grounded in the text.

## Observation Guidance

- Look for whether students orient the book correctly.
- Notice whether students can identify at least one book part consistently.
- Track whether students use picture or text detail when answering questions.
- Record which students need additional language, attention, or motor support for shared reading routines.

## Accessibility Notes

- Use large visuals and predictable routines.
- Offer multimodal responses: pointing, speaking, selecting pictures, and acting out.
- Keep oral directions short and repeated.
- Choose inclusive texts and avoid examples that assume one kind of family or home literacy experience.
