## Unit Purpose

- What is the unit about?
  - Students learn how books and print work while participating in interactive read-alouds that build comprehension, vocabulary, and classroom language routines.
- Why does it matter for learners?
  - Early literacy depends on both oral language and growing understanding that print carries meaning in structured ways. This unit builds those foundations without reducing kindergarten literacy to isolated drills.

## Desired Results

### Students should understand:

- books have parts and are handled in consistent ways
- print is organized and carries language and meaning
- readers and listeners ask and answer questions about what they hear and see in a text

### Students should know:

- front cover, back cover, title, author, and illustrator
- that print moves left to right and top to bottom in English text
- that words are made of letters and are separated by spaces in print

### Students should be able to do:

- orient a book correctly and identify basic book parts
- track print during a shared reading or read-aloud
- ask and answer simple questions about key details in a read-aloud
- use new vocabulary from the text during discussion and response

## Essential Questions

- How do we know how to hold and read a book?
- How does print help us understand a story or idea?
- What do good listeners and readers do when they want to understand more?

  - show how to hold and open a familiar book
  - identify front cover, title, and where reading begins on a page
  - answer and ask at least one question about a read-aloud using words or pictures from the text

### Formative evidence

- observation during shared reading
- oral responses during turn-and-talk
- teacher notes on book handling and print tracking
- quick picture-supported exit responses or retells

## Lesson Sequence

### Lesson 1

- objective: Identify basic book parts and demonstrate how print is followed during a shared read-aloud.
- key activities:
  - introduce a picture book and model how to hold and open it
  - name front cover, title, author, and illustrator
  - perform an interactive read-aloud with finger tracking on selected pages
- evidence of learning:
  - students point to book parts
  - students show where reading starts on a page

### Lesson 2

- objective: Recognize that words are separated by spaces and that print moves left to right and top to bottom.
- key activities:
  - shared sentence strip work with pointer tracking
  - compare a line of print to a picture
  - choral participation during repeated reading of a patterned text
- evidence of learning:
  - students track print direction
  - students identify spaces between words

### Lesson 3

- objective: Ask and answer questions about key details in a read-aloud using oral language, gestures, and pictures.
- key activities:
  - read-aloud with planned stopping points
  - question prompts using picture supports
  - oral retell or sequencing with visuals
- evidence of learning:
  - students respond to key-detail questions
  - students contribute at least one question or clarifying comment

## Accessibility and Inclusion Notes

- Representation supports:
  - use large-format visuals, picture cues, gestures, and modeled pointing to print
- Engagement supports:
  - short, predictable routines, movement, repeated language, and partner talk
- Action/expression supports:
  - allow oral response, pointing, picture selection, acting out, and sentence stems
- Language/accessibility considerations:
  - support multilingual learners with visual vocabulary, repeated phrasing, and explicit oral language routines
- Inclusive examples or framing notes:
  - choose read-aloud texts and examples that avoid narrow family or cultural assumptions and reflect varied identities respectfully

## Subject-Specific Quality Notes

- What discipline-specific risks need attention?
  - tasks that become disconnected from real text use
  - early-literacy work that overemphasizes worksheets instead of oral language and meaningful print interaction
  - text selection that is weak in quality or developmentally mismatched
- What content or task types require extra review?
  - read-aloud text choice
  - vocabulary and question prompts
  - any visual supports that could unintentionally confuse print conventions
