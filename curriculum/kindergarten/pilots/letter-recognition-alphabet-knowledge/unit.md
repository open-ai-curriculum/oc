## Unit Purpose

- What is the unit about?
  - Students build familiarity with letter names, letter shapes, and common sound connections through songs, sorting, tracing, and oral language routines.
- Why does it matter for learners?
  - Kindergarten students need repeated, meaningful encounters with letters and sounds, but the work should stay developmentally appropriate, multimodal, and connected to oral language rather than becoming rote visual drill only.

## Desired Results

### Students should understand:

- letters have names and visual forms
- uppercase and lowercase letters can represent the same alphabet letter
- some letters are connected to common sounds used in spoken words

### Students should know:

- names of a growing set of uppercase and lowercase letters
- that print is made of letters arranged in words
- that a letter can be noticed by its shape and discussed by its name and sound

### Students should be able to do:

- identify named letters in a small taught set
- match uppercase and lowercase versions of taught letters
- produce a common sound for familiar taught letters with support
- sort letters by feature, name, or sound during guided routines

## Essential Questions

- How do we know which letter we are looking at?
- How can a capital letter and a lowercase letter belong together?
- How do letters help us say and read words?

## Assessment Evidence

### Summative evidence

- Students participate in a brief teacher conference or small-group routine in which they:
  - identify several taught letters by name
  - match uppercase and lowercase letter pairs
  - produce a common sound for at least one taught letter with support

### Formative evidence

- teacher observation during letter sorting and naming
- oral participation in alphabet songs and chants
- quick matching tasks with magnetic or paper letters
- movement-based checks such as finding or holding up a named letter

## Lesson Sequence

### Lesson 1

- objective: Recognize and name a small set of taught uppercase and lowercase letters and notice their visual features.
- key activities:
  - introduce two or three focus letters with large visual cards
  - compare uppercase and lowercase forms
  - practice identifying letters in an alphabet chant and matching routine
- evidence of learning:
  - students identify a focus letter by name
  - students match uppercase and lowercase forms with support

### Lesson 2

- objective: Connect taught letters to a common sound through oral examples and picture support.
- key activities:
  - say letter names and repeat a common sound
  - sort pictures by beginning sound for a taught letter
  - practice movement or gesture cues for each focus letter
- evidence of learning:
  - students connect a picture or word to a focus letter sound
  - students join sound routines orally or with gesture

### Lesson 3

- objective: Use taught letters during sorting, naming, and early word-noticing routines.
- key activities:
  - letter hunt in a shared text or chart
  - partner matching game with taught letters
  - oral discussion about how letters show up in names and words
- evidence of learning:
  - students locate or sort taught letters in context
  - students use letter names during guided routines

## Accessibility and Inclusion Notes

- Representation supports:
  - use large letter cards, clear fonts, picture cues, and repeated modeling
- Engagement supports:
  - songs, movement, chants, and hands-on matching tasks
- Action/expression supports:
  - allow pointing, saying, tracing, matching, and holding up letters as evidence
- Language/accessibility considerations:
  - keep directions short, provide oral rehearsal, and avoid fine-motor-heavy tasks as the only response path
- Inclusive examples or framing notes:
  - use names, objects, and examples that reflect varied identities and classroom experiences respectfully

## Subject-Specific Quality Notes

- What discipline-specific risks need attention?
  - isolated memorization with weak meaning or language support
  - visually confusing font choices or letter examples
  - moving too fast from recognition into abstract worksheet completion
- What content or task types require extra review?
  - selected letter/sound examples
  - font and visual design of letter cards
  - picture examples used for sound matching
