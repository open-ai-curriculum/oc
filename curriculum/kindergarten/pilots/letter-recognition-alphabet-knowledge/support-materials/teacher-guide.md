## Alignment Notes

- This guide supports the unit goals around letter naming, matching uppercase and lowercase forms, and connecting letters to common sounds.
- It keeps the work interactive and developmentally appropriate rather than reducing alphabet knowledge to copying or isolated worksheets.
- The guide is intended to improve teacher usability without lowering the literacy target.

## Teacher Preparation Notes

- Choose a small focus set of letters with visually distinct forms for the lesson sequence.
- Use clear, legible fonts and consistent visual materials.
- Prepare picture examples that fit the taught sound clearly and are familiar to learners.

## Likely Misconceptions Or Developmental Challenges

- Students may match letters by partial shape similarity rather than by full recognition.
- Students may know a song sequence without recognizing individual letters in isolation.
- Some picture examples may be confusing if the sound match is not clear in classroom speech.
- Some students may need repeated visual and oral exposure before naming a letter confidently.

## Recommended Teacher Moves

- Teach only a small set at a time and revisit it often.
- Name the letter, show the shape, and invite students to repeat and match.
- Use consistent language such as “This is uppercase M; this is lowercase m.”
- Keep tracing or writing optional at first rather than the main evidence source.

## Observation Guidance

- Notice whether students can identify a named letter in the taught set.
- Track whether students can match uppercase and lowercase forms.
- Note whether students rely only on sequence memory or can recognize the actual letter.
- Watch which students need visual, oral, or tactile support.

## Accessibility Notes

- Use large visuals, clear print, and uncluttered materials.
- Allow pointing, holding, matching, saying, and tracing as response modes.
- Keep oral directions short and repeated.
- Avoid requiring fine motor writing as the only way to participate.
