## Unit Purpose

- What is the unit about?
  - Students listen to familiar stories, talk about what happened, and practice retelling key events in order with oral language, gestures, and picture supports.
- Why does it matter for learners?
  - Kindergarten literacy depends on strong listening comprehension and expressive language. Story retell helps students organize meaning, build vocabulary, and participate more fully in later reading and writing work.

## Desired Results

### Students should understand:

- stories have events that can be told in order
- listeners and speakers use words, pictures, and gestures to share meaning
- retelling helps readers remember and understand what happened

### Students should know:

- beginning, middle, and end in a familiar story sequence
- words such as character, event, first, next, and last
- that a retell should include key events rather than unrelated details

### Students should be able to do:

- answer simple questions about a story read aloud
- retell key events from a familiar story in order with support
- use picture cards or oral sentence stems to describe what happened
- participate in a short partner or whole-group discussion about a story

## Essential Questions

- How do we remember what happened in a story?
- What makes a retell clear?
- How can talking help us understand a story better?

## Assessment Evidence

### Summative evidence

- Students participate in a teacher conference or small-group retell in which they:
  - sequence three key story events using picture cards
  - orally retell the story using first/next/last language with support
  - answer at least one key-detail question about the text

### Formative evidence

- oral responses during read-aloud stopping points
- partner turn-and-talk retells
- teacher notes on sequencing and vocabulary use
- gesture-supported or picture-supported story retells

## Lesson Sequence

### Lesson 1

- objective: Retell a familiar story with support by naming key events in order.
- key activities:
  - interactive read-aloud of a simple narrative text
  - identify beginning, middle, and end with visual cards
  - sequence story events together and practice a short oral retell
- evidence of learning:
  - students place or point to events in sequence
  - students use oral language or gestures to retell at least part of the story

### Lesson 2

- objective: Answer and ask simple questions about key story details.
- key activities:
  - reread the story with planned stopping points
  - ask who, what, and what happened next questions
  - use picture support for discussion and retell
- evidence of learning:
  - students answer key-detail questions
  - students contribute one clarifying question or comment

### Lesson 3

- objective: Use transition words such as first, next, and last in a supported retell.
- key activities:
  - model a three-part retell with picture cards
  - partner practice with sentence stems
  - whole-group retell using gestures and shared language
- evidence of learning:
  - students use sequencing language in a supported retell
  - students connect oral language to the actual story events

## Accessibility and Inclusion Notes

- Representation supports:
  - use picture cards, gestures, repeated oral modeling, and clear visual sequencing supports
- Engagement supports:
  - short story routines, predictable discussion prompts, movement, and partner talk
- Action/expression supports:
  - allow oral response, pointing, acting out, picture placement, and sentence stems
- Language/accessibility considerations:
  - support multilingual learners through repeated language, modeling, visuals, and home-language-friendly discussion where possible
- Inclusive examples or framing notes:
  - choose narrative texts and examples that reflect varied identities and family structures respectfully

## Subject-Specific Quality Notes

- What discipline-specific risks need attention?
  - prompts that are too generic to anchor students to the actual story
  - retell work that becomes pure memory drill without comprehension
  - texts that are weak in quality or too complex for the support level
- What content or task types require extra review?
  - selected read-aloud text
  - sequencing picture cards
  - sentence stems used for retell and discussion
