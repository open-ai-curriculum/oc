## Unit Purpose

- What is the unit about?
  - Students listen closely to spoken language and practice hearing rhyme, producing rhyme, clapping syllables, and noticing beginning sounds through playful oral routines.
- Why does it matter for learners?
  - Phonological awareness supports early reading development, but in kindergarten it should remain oral, interactive, and developmentally appropriate rather than becoming abstract drill work.

## Desired Results

### Students should understand:

- words can sound alike in meaningful patterns such as rhyme
- spoken words can be broken into parts like syllables
- listening carefully to sounds helps readers and speakers notice language patterns

### Students should know:

- what it means for two words to rhyme
- that some words have one beat or syllable and some have more
- that beginning sounds can be heard and compared in spoken words

### Students should be able to do:

- identify whether two spoken words rhyme
- produce a simple rhyming word in songs, poems, or oral routines
- clap and count syllables in familiar spoken words
- sort or discuss words by beginning sound with teacher support

## Essential Questions

- How do words sound the same?
- How can we listen for parts in words?
- How does playing with sounds help us become stronger readers and speakers?

## Assessment Evidence

### Summative evidence

- Students participate in a brief teacher conference or small-group routine in which they:
  - identify a rhyming pair from oral choices
  - clap the syllables in familiar spoken words
  - sort or name one word by beginning sound with teacher prompting

### Formative evidence

- oral participation in songs, chants, and poems
- teacher notes during rhyme sorting and syllable clapping
- quick movement-based checks such as thumbs up for rhyme or stepping syllables
- partner sound-play routines

## Lesson Sequence

### Lesson 1

- objective: Notice and identify rhyming words in songs, chants, and read-aloud language.
- key activities:
  - sing or chant a short rhyme-rich text
  - listen for pairs of words that sound alike at the end
  - sort picture cards into rhyme and not-rhyme examples with support
- evidence of learning:
  - students identify a rhyming pair orally or with gesture
  - students participate in repeating rhyme patterns

### Lesson 2

- objective: Clap and count syllables in familiar names and vocabulary.
- key activities:
  - model syllable clapping with student names and classroom objects
  - use movement to step or tap syllables
  - compare one-syllable and multi-syllable words
- evidence of learning:
  - students clap syllables with growing accuracy
  - students notice that words can have different numbers of beats

### Lesson 3

- objective: Notice and compare beginning sounds in familiar spoken words.
- key activities:
  - sort picture cards by beginning sound
  - say a short line of alliterative words
  - use oral prompts to compare two familiar words
- evidence of learning:
  - students sort or identify words by beginning sound with support
  - students use repeated language to join sound-play routines

## Accessibility and Inclusion Notes

- Representation supports:
  - use picture cards, gestures, movement, and repeated oral models
- Engagement supports:
  - songs, chants, call-and-response, and short playful routines
- Action/expression supports:
  - allow pointing, clapping, stepping, speaking, and picture selection as evidence
- Language/accessibility considerations:
  - keep directions brief, support multilingual learners through repetition and visual cues, and avoid requiring fine motor writing for sound tasks
- Inclusive examples or framing notes:
  - use names, objects, and examples that reflect varied identities and classroom experiences respectfully

## Subject-Specific Quality Notes

- What discipline-specific risks need attention?
  - tasks that shift too quickly from oral phonological awareness into print-heavy work
  - repetitive drills that reduce engagement without improving listening
  - examples that are developmentally awkward or phonetically confusing
- What content or task types require extra review?
  - rhyme examples
  - beginning-sound sorting items
  - oral routines that depend too heavily on one dialect or pronunciation pattern
