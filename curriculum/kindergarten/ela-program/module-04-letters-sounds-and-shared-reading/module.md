## Module Purpose

- What is the module about?
  - Students strengthen uppercase/lowercase automaticity, connect taught letters to common sounds, track concept of word in shared reading, and begin simple sound-linked labeling and dictated writing.
- Why does it matter for learners?
  - Kindergarten needs an explicit foundational-skills spine that remains connected to meaningful shared text and oral language. This module builds that bridge without expecting premature independent reading.

## Desired Results

### Students should understand:

- letters connect to common sounds in spoken words
- uppercase and lowercase letters can represent the same alphabet letter
- shared text is made of words with spaces and can be reread
- sounds can help us label and write simple words with support

### Students should know:

- names and common sounds for a growing taught set of letters
- that words in shared text can be tracked from left to right
- that some high-frequency routine words appear again and again in familiar text
- that labels and dictated sentences connect talk to print

### Students should be able to do:

- identify and match taught letters with greater fluency
- connect pictures and spoken words to taught letter sounds
- point to and reread short shared text with support
- label pictures or contribute sounds and letters in shared writing

## Essential Questions

- How do letters help us read and write words?
- How do we follow words in shared reading?
- How can we use sounds to help make labels and simple writing?

## Assessment Evidence

### Summative evidence

- Students complete a short end-of-module conference in which they:
  - identify and match taught uppercase/lowercase letters
  - connect at least one taught letter to a common sound and picture
  - track a short shared line of text
  - contribute one sound or letter in a labeling or dictated-writing task

### Formative evidence

- daily letter-name and sound quick checks
- shared-reading pointer tracking
- picture sorts and letter hunts
- teacher observation during labeling and sentence-building routines

## Accessibility and Inclusion Notes

- Representation supports:
  - large-print shared text, clear fonts, letter cards, picture cues, and explicit oral modeling
- Engagement supports:
  - short repeated routines, chants, pointer tracking, partner practice, and hands-on card work
- Action/expression supports:
  - pointing, matching, saying, tracing, selecting, dictating, and teacher-recorded writing contributions are valid
- Language/accessibility considerations:
  - keep directions brief, orally rehearse before writing, and avoid fine-motor demands as the only evidence path
- Inclusive examples or framing notes:
  - use names, objects, and print examples that reflect varied classroom experiences respectfully

## Subject-Specific Quality Notes

- What discipline-specific risks need attention?
  - overloading students with too many letters or patterns at once
  - letting print drills become disconnected from meaningful text
  - pushing invented spelling or decoding beyond kindergarten support levels
- What content or task types require extra review?
  - focus-letter sequence
  - picture examples for common sounds
  - shared-reading text selection
