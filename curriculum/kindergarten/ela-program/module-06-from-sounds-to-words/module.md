## Module Purpose

- What is the module about?
  - Students begin turning taught sounds and letters into supported word reading and word building through blending, segmenting, routine high-frequency words, simple decodable text, and dictated/shared sentence work.
- Why does it matter for learners?
  - Kindergarten needs a careful bridge from sound-and-letter knowledge into actual word reading and writing. This module provides that bridge without assuming independent reading stamina beyond what is developmentally appropriate.

## Desired Results

### Students should understand:

- spoken sounds can be blended into words and segmented apart
- taught letters and sounds can help readers work through simple words
- controlled texts can give readers supported practice with known patterns
- shared sentence work connects reading, sounds, and writing

### Students should know:

- that some simple CVC-like word patterns can be read with taught sounds
- that routine high-frequency words appear often in familiar texts
- that blending means putting sounds together
- that segmenting means pulling sounds apart

### Students should be able to do:

- orally blend and segment simple words with support
- read or approximate a small set of controlled words tied to taught patterns
- reread a very short controlled or patterned text with teacher support
- contribute sounds, letters, or words in shared sentence construction

## Essential Questions

- How do sounds come together to make a word?
- How can letters help us read a simple word?
- How do readers and writers use what they know about sounds and words in sentences?

## Assessment Evidence

### Summative evidence

- Students complete a short end-of-module conference in which they:
  - blend a simple set of taught sounds into a word
  - segment a familiar simple word into parts
  - read or identify a controlled word from the taught set
  - contribute one sound, letter, or word to a dictated/shared sentence

### Formative evidence

- oral blending and segmenting routines
- decodable or controlled-text tracking
- routine word checks
- teacher notes during shared sentence construction

## Accessibility and Inclusion Notes

- Representation supports:
  - sound boxes, picture cues, letter cards, teacher-modeled tracking, and oral rehearsal
- Engagement supports:
  - short repeated routines, choral reading, partner practice, and hands-on word building
- Action/expression supports:
  - speaking, pointing, tapping, moving counters, selecting letters, dictating, and teacher-recorded writing contributions are valid
- Language/accessibility considerations:
  - keep tasks brief, highly scaffolded, and grounded in taught patterns; avoid requiring sustained independent reading
- Inclusive examples or framing notes:
  - use familiar vocabulary, names, and images that reflect varied identities and classroom experiences respectfully

## Subject-Specific Quality Notes

- What discipline-specific risks need attention?
  - moving too fast from isolated sound work into unsupported decoding
  - using decodable text that is semantically flat or awkward
  - overburdening students with handwriting when the goal is sound-symbol understanding
- What content or task types require extra review?
  - controlled-word set
  - decodable text quality
  - routine high-frequency word selection
