# K-1 Program Articulation Draft

## Purpose

Define the intended progression from Kindergarten into Grade 1 across the assembled program layer, with emphasis on literacy, math, science, and the shared teacher-use system.

## Status

Draft articulation. This document defines the current intended progression, but it does not replace human-reviewed scope-and-sequence decisions.

## Design Principles

- Move from oral, visual, and concrete experience toward more explicit reading, writing, and explanation.
- Preserve play, talk, observation, movement, and manipulatives as legitimate evidence sources in both grades.
- Increase independence gradually while keeping teacher mediation and accessibility supports visible.
- Keep assessment tied to lesson evidence, not isolated test-only performance.
- Treat literacy, math, science, and the broader subjects as one coherent program rather than isolated packets.

## Literacy Progression

### Kindergarten

- Students orient to books, print, story order, rhyme, and letter knowledge.
- Most literacy work remains oral, shared, gesture-supported, and teacher-mediated.
- Writing-like work is limited to drawing, dictation, labeling, tracing, and short oral rehearsal.

### Grade 1

- Students move into explicit narrative and informational comprehension, phonics/decoding, rereading for fluency, and early opinion/informative writing.
- Reading and writing tasks ask students to connect claims, details, and facts more directly to text or shared content.
- Students begin to sustain short written responses while still using oral rehearsal, sentence frames, and shared modeling.

### K-1 Bridge

- Story retell in Kindergarten becomes text-based retell and key-detail explanation in Grade 1.
- Letter recognition and oral sound work in Kindergarten become decoding and rereading in Grade 1.
- Shared oral explanation in Kindergarten becomes short opinion and informative writing in Grade 1.

## Math Progression

### Kindergarten

- Students count objects with one-to-one correspondence.
- Students connect the last number counted to quantity.
- Students compare small sets and use concrete objects heavily.

### Grade 1

- Students interpret join and separate number stories.
- Students represent addition and subtraction with objects, drawings, and early equations.
- Students explain how they solved a problem, not only what the answer was.

### K-1 Bridge

- Counting and comparing in Kindergarten becomes modeling and explaining number stories in Grade 1.
- Concrete set work remains important, but Grade 1 expects more explicit strategy explanation.

## Science Progression

### Kindergarten

- Students observe visible features of weather and record simple evidence with symbols, pictures, and talk.
- Science is grounded in noticing, naming, and shared recording.

### Grade 1

- Students observe plants, identify needs, and record simple evidence tied to growth and patterns.
- Science begins to ask for a clearer relationship between observation and explanation.

### K-1 Bridge

- Kindergarten builds the habit of careful noticing.
- Grade 1 extends that habit into simple evidence-based statements about living things and patterns.

## Broader Subject Progression

### Social Studies

- Kindergarten focuses on helpers, roles, family, school, and community awareness.
- Grade 1 adds maps, shared places, and basic citizenship responsibilities.

### Arts

- Kindergarten emphasizes expression and sharing.
- Grade 1 adds explanation of artistic choices and response to prompts.

### Health

- Kindergarten emphasizes naming feelings and safe choices.
- Grade 1 adds explanation of why a health or safety choice is helpful.

### Physical Education

- Kindergarten emphasizes control, stopping, balancing, and safe participation.
- Grade 1 adds cue-following, locomotor combinations, and cooperative participation.

### Technology

- Kindergarten emphasizes simple sequencing and tool use.
- Grade 1 adds clearer step ordering, algorithmic language, and safe digital-use norms.

## Program-Layer Expectations Across Both Grades

Every full program should include:

- student-facing textbook content
- workbook or practice content
- teacher-edition guidance
- grading-key guidance
- assessment-pack guidance
- packet-level support materials
- packet-level classroom materials
- assembled reproducibles and exemplar-response references

## Draft Cross-Grade Success Markers

By the end of Kindergarten, students should be ready to:

- listen and respond to shared texts
- retell simple events in order
- identify some letters and hear simple sound patterns
- count and compare small sets
- observe and describe familiar phenomena

By the end of Grade 1, students should be ready to:

- answer text-based questions about stories and informational texts
- decode and reread simple connected text with growing control
- produce short opinion and informative writing
- solve and explain addition and subtraction situations within the taught scope
- record and explain simple observations across content areas
