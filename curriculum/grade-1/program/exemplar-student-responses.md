# Grade 1 Exemplar Student Responses

## Purpose

Provide assembled examples of what draft Grade 1 student responses may look like across the program layer so teachers can calibrate expectations for explanation, evidence, and early independent work.

## Usage Notes

- These exemplars are illustrative drafts, not final calibrated anchors.
- Grade 1 evidence may still be oral, shared, drawn, or partially dictated.
- Score for understanding first, then note independence and prompting separately.

## Literacy Exemplars

### Narrative Reading

- Student says, "The important detail is that the boy lost the key because that is the problem."
- Student retells, "First he looked in the house. Next he checked his pocket. Last he found it by the door."

### Informational Reading

- Student identifies the topic as `frogs` and answers, "A frog starts as a tadpole."

### Phonics and Fluency

- Student reads `map`, `sit`, `hop`, then rereads a short line more smoothly on the second attempt.

### Opinion Writing

- Student writes, "My opinion is recess should be longer. One reason is we get more time to play."

### Informative Writing

- Student writes, "Plants need water. Plants also need light."

## Non-Literacy Exemplars

### Addition and Subtraction

- Student draws five circles, crosses out two, writes `5 - 2 = 3`, and says, "Three are left."

### Plants, Needs, and Patterns

- Student writes, "The plant is taller today. It needs water and light."

### Community, Maps, and Citizenship

- Student circles the library on a simple map and says, "People should take care of books."

### Arts, Performance, and Creation

- Student says, "I used red to show excitement."

### Nutrition, Safety, and Emotions

- Student says, "Wearing a helmet is safe because it protects your head."

### Locomotor Skills and Cooperation

- Student follows run / stop / hop cues and waits for a partner turn.

### Digital Citizenship and Algorithmic Thinking

- Student orders three step cards and says, "First log in, next open the program, last close the device safely."

## Teacher Reminder

- Use exemplars to calibrate the type and quality of evidence, not to force one exact response.
- Keep track of whether the student worked independently, with prompting, or through shared composition.
