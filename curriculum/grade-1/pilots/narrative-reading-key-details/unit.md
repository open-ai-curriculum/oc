## Unit Purpose

- What is the unit about?
  - Students read or hear narrative texts, identify key details, answer text-grounded questions, and retell stories with beginning, middle, and end.
- Why does it matter for learners?
  - Grade 1 students need to move from supported kindergarten retell into clearer evidence-based comprehension. This unit strengthens story understanding, oral expression, and text-grounded talk.

## Desired Results

### Students should understand:

- stories contain key details that help readers understand what happens
- retelling should include important events in order rather than every detail
- readers use the text and pictures to answer questions and explain what happened

### Students should know:

- the meaning of key detail, retell, beginning, middle, and end
- that story elements can be remembered and discussed in sequence
- that readers answer questions using what the text says and shows

### Students should be able to do:

- answer questions about key details in a Grade 1-appropriate narrative text
- retell a story in sequence using oral language and simple text evidence
- identify important events rather than unrelated or favorite-only details
- use vocabulary from the story in discussion and retell

## Essential Questions

- How do we know which details in a story are important?
- What makes a retell clear and complete?
- How do pictures and words help us answer questions about a story?

## Assessment Evidence

### Summative evidence

- Students complete a teacher conference or small-group retell in which they:
  - answer two questions about key story details
  - sequence the beginning, middle, and end of a familiar story
  - retell the story orally using at least one story-specific detail

### Formative evidence

- turn-and-talk responses during reading
- teacher notes on key-detail responses
- sequence-card routines
- oral retell using sentence stems and picture supports

## Lesson Sequence

### Lesson 1

- objective: Identify key details in a narrative text and explain how they help tell what happened.
- key activities:
  - read aloud or shared read a short narrative text
  - stop at selected points to ask who, what, and where questions
  - sort details into important and not-as-important with teacher support
- evidence of learning:
  - students answer a key-detail question using words or pictures from the story
  - students discuss why one detail matters more than another

### Lesson 2

- objective: Retell a story in sequence using beginning, middle, and end language.
- key activities:
  - sequence three to five events from the text
  - practice oral retell with a partner using transition words
  - compare a strong retell and a weak retell
- evidence of learning:
  - students place events in order
  - students use sequencing language in a supported retell

### Lesson 3

- objective: Answer and ask questions about a story using text and picture details.
- key activities:
  - reread a familiar story section
  - ask questions that require students to return to the text or illustrations
  - share oral responses and explain how the answer was found
- evidence of learning:
  - students answer questions with text-based support
  - students ask one relevant question about the story

## Accessibility and Inclusion Notes

- Representation supports:
  - use clear illustrations, sequencing cards, highlighted text details, and modeled retells
- Engagement supports:
  - predictable reading routines, partner talk, movement to sequence events, and repeated rereading
- Action/expression supports:
  - allow oral response, pointing, picture placement, annotated retell cards, and sentence stems
- Language/accessibility considerations:
  - support multilingual learners with explicit vocabulary teaching, repeated oral models, and visuals tied directly to the story
- Inclusive examples or framing notes:
  - select stories and examples that avoid narrow cultural assumptions and reflect varied experiences respectfully

## Subject-Specific Quality Notes

- What discipline-specific risks need attention?
  - prompts that can be answered without real reading or listening
  - retells that reward fluency but not comprehension
  - weak text selection or pictures that do not support the comprehension target
- What content or task types require extra review?
  - selected narrative text
  - question sets and retell stems
  - important-vs-unimportant detail sorting tasks
