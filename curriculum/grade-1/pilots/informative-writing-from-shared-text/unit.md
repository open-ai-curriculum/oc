## Unit Purpose

- What is the unit about?
  - Students use a shared informational text or topic study to name a topic, state simple facts, and produce brief informative writing with teacher support.
- Why does it matter for learners?
  - Grade 1 students need a bridge from reading informational texts to writing about what they learned. This unit helps them turn shared reading and discussion into simple factual writing rather than unsupported opinion or copied sentences.

## Desired Results

### Students should understand:

- informative writing tells readers about a topic using facts
- readers and writers can use a shared text to learn and then explain
- drawing, talking, writing, and rereading all help make information clearer

### Students should know:

- the meaning of topic, fact, and detail
- that informative writing can start by naming the topic
- that facts in writing should match what was learned from the text or shared study

### Students should be able to do:

- name a shared topic in speech and writing
- state one or more facts connected to the topic
- use drawing, dictation, or writing to communicate what was learned
- reread a short informative piece and add or clarify a fact with support

## Essential Questions

- How do writers teach others about a topic?
- How do we know if a fact belongs with the topic?
- How can reading and talking help us write clearly?

## Assessment Evidence

### Summative evidence

- Students produce a short informative piece in which they:
  - name a shared topic
  - include at least one fact learned from a shared text or lesson
  - reread the piece with teacher support and clarify one part if needed

### Formative evidence

- oral rehearsal of factual sentences
- turn-and-talk about what was learned from the shared text
- teacher notes on whether facts match the topic
- shared or interactive writing of topic-and-fact sentences

## Lesson Sequence

### Lesson 1

- objective: Name a shared topic and write one fact learned from a shared informational text.
- key activities:
  - reread or revisit a short shared informational text
  - identify the topic and one or two facts together
  - orally rehearse and then write or dictate a topic-and-fact sentence
- evidence of learning:
  - students name the topic clearly
  - students write or dictate a fact that matches the topic

### Lesson 2

- objective: Add another fact or detail that helps teach about the topic.
- key activities:
  - reread a model informative piece
  - discuss which details help the reader learn more
  - add another fact with teacher support
- evidence of learning:
  - students extend a piece beyond a single sentence
  - students add a fact connected to the topic

### Lesson 3

- objective: Reread and revise a short informative piece for clarity.
- key activities:
  - reread writing aloud with a partner or teacher
  - notice where a fact, word, or label is missing
  - revise by adding or clarifying information
- evidence of learning:
  - students identify one place to improve a piece
  - students revise with support to make the information clearer

## Accessibility and Inclusion Notes

- Representation supports:
  - use shared texts, pictures, labels, modeled writing, and visible topic/fact organizers
- Engagement supports:
  - choose clear, high-interest shared topics, allow talk before writing, and keep writing tasks short and purposeful
- Action/expression supports:
  - allow drawing, dictation, shared writing, oral rehearsal, and teacher-supported sentence construction
- Language/accessibility considerations:
  - support multilingual learners with repeated oral frames, visuals tied directly to the topic, and flexible pathways from speaking to writing
- Inclusive examples or framing notes:
  - use shared texts and topics that reflect varied people, places, and experiences respectfully

## Subject-Specific Quality Notes

- What discipline-specific risks need attention?
  - writing that becomes copied text instead of knowledge expression
  - facts that drift away from the shared topic or source
  - prompts that do not clearly distinguish fact from opinion
  - revision expectations that are too abstract for early Grade 1 writers
- What content or task types require extra review?
  - selected shared texts or topic-study materials
  - topic/fact sentence frames and model pieces
  - revision expectations for informative writing
