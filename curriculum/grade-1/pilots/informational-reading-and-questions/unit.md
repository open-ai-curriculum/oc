## Unit Purpose

- What is the unit about?
  - Students read or hear short informational texts, identify key details, ask and answer questions, and explain what the text is mostly about.
- Why does it matter for learners?
  - Grade 1 students need practice reading for information, not just stories. This unit helps them notice important facts, use text features and illustrations, and talk about what they learned from a text.

## Desired Results

### Students should understand:

- informational texts give facts and details about a topic
- good readers ask and answer questions to understand more
- key details help explain what the text is mostly about

### Students should know:

- the meaning of topic, key detail, question, and answer
- that headings, pictures, and labels can help readers learn from a text
- that readers use what the text says and shows to answer questions

### Students should be able to do:

- ask and answer questions about key details in an informational text
- identify what the text is mostly about in simple language
- use a picture, heading, or sentence from the text to support an answer
- talk or write briefly about something learned from the text

## Essential Questions

- How do we learn from an informational text?
- Which details in a text are most important?
- How can pictures, headings, and words help us answer questions?

## Assessment Evidence

### Summative evidence

- Students complete a teacher conference or small-group text discussion in which they:
  - answer two questions about key details in a short informational text
  - say what the text is mostly about
  - point to or name a picture, heading, or sentence that helped them answer

### Formative evidence

- oral responses during shared reading
- teacher notes on key-detail questions
- simple graphic or verbal “main idea / detail” routines
- partner talk about what was learned from a text section

## Lesson Sequence

### Lesson 1

- objective: Ask and answer questions about key details in a short informational text.
- key activities:
  - shared reading or read-aloud of a short informational text
  - pause to ask who, what, where, and how questions tied to the text
  - identify one detail that helps explain the topic
- evidence of learning:
  - students answer a key-detail question using the text or picture
  - students identify a useful detail from the reading

### Lesson 2

- objective: Identify what a text is mostly about using key details.
- key activities:
  - reread a short text section
  - sort details that fit the main topic
  - practice saying “This text is mostly about...”
- evidence of learning:
  - students identify a simple main topic
  - students connect a detail to that topic

### Lesson 3

- objective: Use text features and illustrations to answer questions.
- key activities:
  - notice headings, labels, and pictures
  - ask where to look for an answer in the text
  - discuss how a feature helped understanding
- evidence of learning:
  - students use a feature or picture to support an answer
  - students explain how the feature helped

## Accessibility and Inclusion Notes

- Representation supports:
  - use clear illustrations, headings, labels, and modeled think-alouds
- Engagement supports:
  - short text sections, predictable question routines, partner talk, and movement to show answers
- Action/expression supports:
  - allow oral response, pointing, simple notes or drawings, and discussion stems
- Language/accessibility considerations:
  - support multilingual learners with explicit vocabulary teaching, visuals tied directly to the text, and repeated oral language frames
- Inclusive examples or framing notes:
  - select informational texts that avoid narrow cultural assumptions and reflect a range of people, places, and topics respectfully

## Subject-Specific Quality Notes

- What discipline-specific risks need attention?
  - questions that can be answered without reading or listening carefully
  - texts with weak coherence or unclear topic focus
  - overreliance on pictures without connecting them to the text
- What content or task types require extra review?
  - selected informational text
  - question sets and topic/detail routines
  - text feature tasks involving headings, labels, and illustrations
