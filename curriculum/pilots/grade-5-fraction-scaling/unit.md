## Unit Purpose

- What is the unit about?
  - Students develop meaning for multiplying fractions by using visual models, reasoning about part-of-a-part, and interpreting multiplication as scaling.
- Why does it matter for learners?
  - Fraction multiplication is a gateway concept for ratio, proportional reasoning, algebraic thinking, and realistic quantitative problem solving.

## Desired Results

### Students should understand:

- multiplying by a fraction can reduce, preserve, or enlarge a quantity depending on the factor
- area models can represent fraction multiplication and explain why procedures work
- equivalent representations and reasoned explanations are part of doing mathematics, not an extra afterthought

### Students should know:

- vocabulary for factor, product, numerator, denominator, unit fraction, and area model
- that fraction multiplication can be represented with diagrams, equations, and context problems
- that the standard algorithmic pattern reflects a conceptual structure, not just a rule to memorize

### Students should be able to do:

- represent fraction-by-whole-number and fraction-by-fraction multiplication with area or set models
- explain why a product is reasonable using scaling language
- solve contextual and non-contextual fraction multiplication tasks
- compare incorrect and correct reasoning about fraction products

## Essential Questions

- What does it mean to take a fraction of a fraction?
- Why can multiplying by a number less than 1 make a quantity smaller?
- How do diagrams, equations, and words help us justify a fraction product?

## Assessment Evidence

### Summative evidence

- Students complete a short performance task in which they solve and explain two fraction multiplication problems:
  - one represented with an area model
  - one contextualized as a scaling situation
- Students must justify the product, label the representation, and explain why the result is reasonable.

### Formative evidence

- annotated warm-up responses
- teacher observation during diagram construction and discussion
- mid-lesson explanation prompts
- exit tickets asking students to compare products and explain whether a result should be greater or less than the original quantity

## Lesson Sequence

### Lesson 1

- objective: Interpret fraction multiplication as taking a fractional part of a whole and represent it with area models.
- key activities:
  - launch with a visual prompt comparing `1/2 of 8` and `1/2 of 1/2`
  - build area models for fraction-by-fraction products
  - discuss why the product is smaller than both original unit wholes in selected examples
- evidence of learning:
  - students label models accurately
  - students explain the meaning of numerator and denominator in the product

### Lesson 2

- objective: Interpret multiplication by fractions as scaling and compare the size of products without always calculating exactly.
- key activities:
  - number talk on whether multiplying by `3/4`, `1`, or `5/4` shrinks or enlarges a quantity
  - match equations to scaling statements and diagrams
  - justify estimates before exact computation
- evidence of learning:
  - students predict direction of change correctly
  - students use scaling language to justify estimates

### Lesson 3

- objective: Solve and explain fraction multiplication problems in context using visual and symbolic reasoning.
- key activities:
  - solve a recipe or area context with mixed representations
  - compare sample student work, including one flawed explanation
  - revise solutions using peer feedback
- evidence of learning:
  - students connect context, diagram, and equation
  - students critique reasoning using mathematical evidence

## Accessibility and Inclusion Notes

- Representation supports:
  - use large, clearly partitioned area models and verbal explanation alongside symbolic notation
- Engagement supports:
  - provide low-floor entry questions and invite multiple valid methods for the same product
- Action/expression supports:
  - allow oral explanation, annotated diagrams, sentence stems, and manipulatives before symbolic formalization
- Language/accessibility considerations:
  - preteach key vocabulary, reduce unnecessary reading load, and ensure visuals are high contrast and uncluttered
- Inclusive examples or framing notes:
  - use familiar, non-stereotyped contexts and avoid culturally narrow assumptions about home experiences

## Subject-Specific Quality Notes

- What discipline-specific risks need attention?

  - procedural shortcuts introduced before conceptual grounding
  - tasks that only ask for final answers without explanation
- What content or task types require extra review?
