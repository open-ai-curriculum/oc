## Lesson Preparation Notes

- Verify all sample models before instruction. Incorrect partitioning will confuse the underlying concept.
- Prepare one correct model and one intentionally flawed model for class discussion.
- Decide in advance which students may benefit from pre-drawn rectangles or manipulatives while still participating in the same reasoning work.

## Likely Misconceptions

- Students may multiply numerators and denominators procedurally without understanding the whole in the diagram.
- Students may treat the overlap region as an arbitrary shaded area instead of the product.
- Students may think multiplication always makes a quantity larger.
- Students may create unequal partitions, which invalidates the area model.

## Recommended Teacher Moves

- Ask, “What whole are we partitioning?” before students begin drawing.
- Prompt students to explain why the overlap region represents “a part of a part.”
- Use comparison questions such as, “Would multiplying by `1/2` make this quantity larger or smaller? Why?”
- When students produce equivalent fractions, press them to connect the diagram to the equivalent symbolic form rather than accepting the answer alone.

## Discussion Prompts

- “What does the denominator tell us about the number of equal parts in the whole?”
- “How do you know the shaded overlap is the product?”
- “Why is `2/3 x 3/4` equal to `6/12`, and why can that also be named `1/2`?”
- “What changes when the factor is greater than 1?”

## Assessment Guidance

- Listen for whether students name the whole consistently.
- Look for reasoning that connects model, equation, and interpretation.
- Treat correct answers without explanation as incomplete evidence when the target is conceptual understanding.
- Use the exit ticket to separate procedural familiarity from scaling understanding.

## Accessibility Notes

- Offer pre-drawn grids or rectangles for learners who need reduced setup demand.
- Use explicit, short verbal directions paired with visible modeling.
- Provide vocabulary supports for numerator, denominator, factor, product, and overlap.
- Ensure visual models are high contrast and large enough for classroom display.
