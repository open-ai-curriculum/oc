- Lesson objective:
  - Students will represent and explain fraction-by-fraction multiplication with area models and use the model to justify why the product is a fractional part of the whole.

## Materials and Preparation

- Materials needed:
  - grid paper or projected rectangles
  - colored pencils or digital annotation tools
  - fraction strips or paper rectangles for optional concrete modeling
  - exit ticket
- Teacher preparation:
  - prepare two worked visual examples, including one with a common partitioning error
  - verify all model partitions and labels for correctness before instruction

## Lesson Flow

### Opening

- How will the lesson activate prior knowledge or frame the task?
  - Ask students to sketch `1/2 of 8` and describe what “of” means.
  - Transition to `1/2 of 1/3` and ask how the same idea could work when both quantities are fractions.

### Core Learning Experience

- What will students do?
  - Students build area models for `1/2 x 1/3`, `2/3 x 3/4`, and one student-chosen example.
  - Students shade one fraction in one direction and the second fraction in another direction, then identify the overlap region as the product.
- What is the key content, text, problem, source, or phenomenon?
  - The central idea is that multiplying fractions can be understood as taking a part of a part, which can be visualized through overlapping partitions of a whole.

### Guided Practice / Discussion / Investigation

- How will students process, practice, or analyze the learning?
  - Pairs compare two models and explain why the denominator of the product reflects the number of equal parts in the full partitioned whole.
  - Students analyze a flawed model where the whole is partitioned inconsistently and explain the mistake.
  - The class discusses when the product should be smaller than the original factors and why.

### Check for Understanding

- What evidence will show whether students are meeting the lesson objective?
  - Students complete an explanation prompt: “Use a diagram and words to show why `2/3 x 3/4 = 6/12 = 1/2`.”
  - Teacher listens for correct use of the whole, equal partitioning, overlap, and equivalence.

### Closing

- How will the lesson synthesize or reflect on the learning?
  - Students complete an exit ticket comparing `3/4 x 1/2` and `3/4 x 3/2` and explain which product is greater and why using scaling language.
