# E1 Intervention Playbook

## Purpose

Map `E1` failure modes to bounded intervention responses.

## Intervention Rules

### For E1-FM-01 Claim-Evidence Disconnect

Primary interventions:

- use two-column supports labeled claim and evidence
- ask the learner to point to the observation before stating the claim
- rehearse sentence frames such as "I know this because I observed"

Expected effect:

- learner links claims to relevant observations more reliably

Reverification target:

- one claim-and-evidence item

### For E1-FM-02 Unsupported Assertion

Primary interventions:

- compare evidence-based and non-evidence-based explanations
- ask what was actually seen measured or recorded
- remove authority language and return to direct observation

Expected effect:

- learner relies less on unsupported assertion

Reverification target:

- one explanation item

### For E1-FM-03 Evidence Selection Error

Primary interventions:

- sort strong and weak evidence for the same claim
- ask which observation most directly answers the question
- contrast relevant with irrelevant details

Expected effect:

- learner selects more relevant evidence for a claim

Reverification target:

- one evidence-selection item

### For E1-FM-04 Context Boundary

Primary interventions:

- repeat evidence-talk routines across plants, motion, weather, and material examples
- preserve the claim-evidence structure while changing the phenomenon
- fade prompts after a successful familiar example

Expected effect:

- learner transfers evidence talk beyond one familiar setting

Reverification target:

- one transfer item

## Escalation Rule

If the same dominant failure mode remains `blocking` after two bounded intervention cycles, escalate to review for language load, discourse-support needs, or prompt-design mismatch rather than intensifying the same local practice blindly.

## Human Review Rule

Interventions may be suggested automatically, but the teacher or human reviewer must remain able to override the recommendation when language access, confidence, or context suggests a different explanation of the learner state.
