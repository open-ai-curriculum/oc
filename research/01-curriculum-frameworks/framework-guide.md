# Curriculum Development Framework Guide

## Purpose

This guide summarizes the core curriculum-design frameworks that currently provide the strongest general foundation for the repository's work.

It is intended to support curriculum architecture, lesson planning, assessment alignment, and review workflows.

## Scope

This document focuses on broad design frameworks rather than subject-specific pedagogy.

The main frameworks emphasized here are:

- Understanding by Design
- Bloom's Taxonomy
- UDL
- culturally responsive design as a review lens

## Core Frameworks

### 1. Understanding by Design

UbD remains one of the strongest general planning structures for this repository because it begins with outcomes and evidence rather than activity selection.

#### Three stages

**Stage 1: Identify desired results**

- what students should know, understand, and be able to do
- what enduring understandings matter
- what transfer goals are intended

**Stage 2: Determine acceptable evidence**

- how learning will be demonstrated
- what counts as evidence of understanding
- what performance tasks or assessments are needed

**Stage 3: Plan learning experiences and instruction**

- what knowledge and skills students need
- what sequence of experiences will build toward the outcome
- what instruction and support are required

#### Why it matters here

UbD is especially useful for:

- keeping standards, instruction, and assessment aligned
- avoiding activity-first curriculum design
- supporting coherent unit architecture

### 2. Bloom's Taxonomy

Bloom's revised taxonomy remains useful as a planning and review tool for cognitive demand.

#### Key levels

- remember
- understand
- apply
- analyze
- evaluate
- create

#### Why it matters here

Bloom's is useful for:

- checking whether objectives and tasks match intended rigor
- avoiding overreliance on recall-level work
- reviewing whether assessments elicit the intended kind of thinking

### 3. Universal Design for Learning

UDL should be treated as a baseline design approach rather than a narrow accommodation strategy.

#### Core UDL lenses

- multiple means of representation
- multiple means of engagement
- multiple means of action and expression

#### Why it matters here

UDL helps the repository design materials that:

- reduce avoidable barriers
- support varied learner access needs
- remain usable across different contexts and formats

### 4. Culturally Responsive and Inclusive Design

This is not treated here as a fully specified framework yet, but it is an essential review lens.

Current implication for the repository:

- examples and framing should reflect diverse learners respectfully
- materials should avoid defaulting to narrow cultural assumptions
- subject-specific work should later deepen this area with stronger source coverage

## Integration Model for This Repository

The current best integrated approach is:

1. use UbD to structure units and lessons
2. use Bloom's to review cognitive demand
3. use UDL to reduce barriers from the start
4. apply culturally responsive and inclusion checks during review
5. use AI only within the governance and human-review rules already defined elsewhere in `research/`

## Practical Implications

This guide supports several immediate repository choices:

- objectives should be explicit before lessons are drafted
- assessment should be planned before or alongside instruction
- accessibility and inclusion should be treated as core quality concerns
- AI-generated materials should be reviewed against real instructional logic, not only readability

## Limits

This guide does not yet provide:

- subject-specific curriculum architecture
- deep learning-science synthesis by discipline
- a full treatment of culturally sustaining pedagogy

Those are addressed elsewhere or remain future work.
