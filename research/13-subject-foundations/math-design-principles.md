# Mathematics Design Principles

## Purpose

This document translates the repository's general curriculum guidance into math-specific design rules for units, lessons, and tasks.

## Core Position

Strong math curriculum should not force a choice between:

- conceptual understanding
- procedural fluency
- application

The repository should design materials that develop all three in a coherent sequence.

## High-Value Design Rules

### 1. Keep the mathematics central

Tasks should require students to engage with the intended mathematical idea, not mainly with decorative context.

Good implication:

- use real contexts when they clarify or motivate mathematics
- avoid wordiness or storytelling that obscures the target concept

### 2. Build coherence across lessons

Math learning should progress through connected ideas.

Implication:

- each lesson should build from prior knowledge toward a visible mathematical objective
- units should not feel like disconnected skill packets

### 3. Use representations intentionally

Representations matter in mathematics learning.

Implication:

- diagrams, tables, graphs, equations, and models should support understanding
- multiple representations should connect, not compete

### 4. Make student reasoning visible

Tasks should often ask students to:

- explain
- justify
- compare methods
- critique reasoning

This is especially important in an AI-enabled environment where answer-only tasks are easier to complete superficially.

### 5. Preserve grade-level demand

Scaffolds should improve access without replacing the core mathematics.

Implication:

- avoid reducing cognitively demanding work to answer-following procedures
- support struggle productively rather than removing the need to think

## Lesson Design Implications

Math lessons in this repository should generally include:

- a clear mathematical objective
- tasks that surface student thinking
- opportunities to connect methods or representations
- checks for understanding tied to the actual target

## Common Quality Failures

- worked examples that are mathematically incorrect
- over-scaffolded tasks that erase reasoning
- assessment items that reward answer retrieval more than understanding
- context-heavy tasks with weak mathematical focus

## Relationship to Review

When reviewing math materials, the highest-priority questions are:

1. Is the math correct?
2. Is the target mathematical idea clear?
3. Does the task require meaningful mathematical thinking?
4. Do the supports preserve grade-level rigor?
