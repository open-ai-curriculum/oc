# Science Design Principles

## Purpose

This document translates the repository's general curriculum guidance into science-specific design rules for units, lessons, and investigations.

## Core Position

Strong science curriculum should help students make sense of phenomena, build explanations, use evidence, and engage with models and practices of science.

It should not collapse into:

- vocabulary memorization
- disconnected facts
- activity sequences with weak conceptual purpose

## High-Value Design Rules

### 1. Organize around meaningful questions or phenomena

Science learning is stronger when students are working to explain, model, or investigate something meaningful.

Implication:

- units and lessons should be anchored in phenomena, questions, or problems where possible

### 2. Connect ideas, practices, and evidence

Science understanding should involve more than recalling terms.

Implication:

- tasks should connect concepts with observation, investigation, modeling, or explanation

### 3. Treat models and representations carefully

Science relies heavily on diagrams, models, and symbolic representations.

Implication:

- visual materials need strong accuracy review
- visual accessibility must be planned, not assumed

### 4. Make student explanation central

Students should often be asked to:

- explain a phenomenon
- interpret evidence
- evaluate a claim
- compare models or explanations

### 5. Preserve conceptual depth

Supports should increase access without reducing science to glossary-level recall.

## Lesson Design Implications

Science lessons in this repository should generally include:

- a clear science objective
- a meaningful question, phenomenon, or evidence source
- structured opportunities for explanation or modeling
- checks for understanding tied to scientific reasoning, not only vocabulary

## Common Quality Failures

- fact-heavy lessons with weak conceptual structure
- investigations disconnected from the target idea
- inaccurate diagrams or explanations
- inaccessible visual or procedural materials

## Relationship to Review

When reviewing science materials, the highest-priority questions are:

1. Is the science accurate?
2. Is the lesson organized around a meaningful scientific idea or problem?
3. Are students asked to use evidence, explanation, or modeling?
4. Are diagrams, representations, and procedures accessible?
