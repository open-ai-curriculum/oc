# Literacy Design Principles

## Purpose

This document translates the repository's general curriculum guidance into literacy and language-arts design rules.

## Core Position

Strong literacy curriculum should build reading, writing, speaking, listening, and language through meaningful engagement with texts, ideas, and evidence.

It should not collapse into:

- disconnected skill drills
- generic discussion prompts
- writing tasks with weak textual grounding

## High-Value Design Rules

### 1. Build knowledge through texts

Texts should do more than supply passages for skill questions.

Implication:

- text sets and lesson sequences should help students build domain knowledge and understanding

### 2. Keep tasks text-grounded

Reading, discussion, and writing tasks should usually require evidence from the text or texts under study.

Implication:

- avoid prompts that can be answered generically with little reading

### 3. Treat writing as thinking

Writing tasks should help students:

- interpret
- explain
- argue
- synthesize

Implication:

- writing should not be treated only as output formatting

### 4. Use discussion purposefully

Speaking and listening tasks should deepen comprehension and reasoning, not merely add participation.

### 5. Attend to language, vocabulary, and access

Curriculum should support comprehension and expression without creating avoidable barriers for multilingual learners or struggling readers.

## Lesson Design Implications

Literacy lessons in this repository should generally include:

- a clear comprehension, language, or writing objective
- purposeful text use
- evidence-based discussion or writing opportunities
- checks for understanding tied to the intended literacy work

## Common Quality Failures

- text-independent prompts
- writing tasks that reward fluency but not thought
- weak control of text complexity
- reading demands that are misaligned to the goal

## Relationship to Review

When reviewing literacy materials, the highest-priority questions are:

1. Does the task require real reading or evidence use?
2. Is the writing or discussion cognitively meaningful?
3. Is the text appropriate in complexity, quality, and purpose?
4. Are language and accessibility supports appropriate?
