# Civics and History Design Principles

## Purpose

This document translates the repository's general curriculum guidance into civics- and history-specific design rules.

## Core Position

Strong civics and history curriculum should center inquiry, evidence, interpretation, and responsible treatment of sensitive material.

It should not collapse into:

- fact-list recitation
- oversimplified narratives
- unsupported “both sides” framing

## High-Value Design Rules

### 1. Make inquiry central

Lessons should often be organized around meaningful historical or civic questions.

Implication:

- students should be asked to investigate, interpret, compare, or argue from evidence

### 2. Distinguish fact, interpretation, and argument

Students need help understanding the difference between:

- established factual claims
- interpretive claims
- arguments supported by evidence

### 3. Use sources deliberately

Source selection matters.

Implication:

- materials should use sources that illuminate perspective, context, and evidence
- source framing should be reviewed carefully for bias and omission

### 4. Handle sensitive content with review discipline

Civics and history often include contested, traumatic, or identity-relevant material.

Implication:

- these materials require heightened bias, representation, and factual review

### 5. Avoid false equivalence

Multiple perspectives do not require treating all claims as equally supported.

## Lesson Design Implications

Civics/history lessons in this repository should generally include:

- a clear inquiry or knowledge objective
- evidence or source engagement
- opportunities for explanation, comparison, or argument
- checks for understanding tied to reasoning and sourcing

## Common Quality Failures

- invented details or quotations
- oversimplified narratives
- shallow “debate” formats with weak evidence expectations
- biased or exclusionary framing

## Relationship to Review

When reviewing civics/history materials, the highest-priority questions are:

1. Is the content factually reliable?
2. Are sources and perspectives handled responsibly?
3. Does the lesson require evidence-based thinking?
4. Is the framing appropriate for sensitive or contested material?
