# Literacy Text and Task Guidance

## Purpose

This document provides a practical review lens for selecting texts and designing tasks in literacy and language-arts materials.

## Core Position

Text quality and task quality are inseparable.

A strong text with weak tasks, or strong prompts with weak texts, will usually produce weak literacy instruction.

## Text Selection Questions

Reviewers and authors should ask:

1. Is the text worth sustained attention?
2. Is the complexity appropriate for the intended learners and support level?
3. Does the text contribute meaningfully to knowledge building or literary study?
4. Are there accessibility or cultural framing issues that need attention?

## Task Design Questions

Tasks should ask students to do meaningful literacy work such as:

- cite evidence
- interpret language or structure
- compare ideas across texts
- explain claims and reasoning
- write or speak with support from reading

## Writing Guidance

Writing prompts should:

- make the purpose clear
- connect directly to the reading or content
- reward reasoning, evidence, and organization
- avoid generic “opinion” prompts detached from text or knowledge

## AI-Era Review Implications

Because generic prompts are easy to answer superficially with AI, literacy tasks should prefer:

- text-dependent prompts
- evidence-based writing
- comparison, analysis, and interpretation
- visible use of sources or quoted support where appropriate

## Common Risks

- prompts answerable without reading
- text sets with weak coherence
- writing tasks that overvalue polish over thinking
- inaccessible supports or reading demands
