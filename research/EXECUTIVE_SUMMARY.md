# Executive Summary: Research Basis for Open Curriculum Development

## Purpose

This summary explains what the current `research/` directory does and does not yet establish as a foundation for the rest of the project.

It is intended to support practical decisions about curriculum architecture, governance, review workflows, and future research investment.

## Current Assessment

The repository now has a credible Phase 1 research base for building the next layer of the project.

It is strong in:

- curriculum design frameworks
- AI governance and human review
- accessibility expectations
- privacy and tool-vetting guidance
- curriculum quality review structures
- interoperability planning

It is not yet a complete or final authority base for all later curriculum development work.

## What the Research Supports with Higher Confidence

### 1. Backward design and aligned curriculum architecture

The research supports using:

- Understanding by Design
- Bloom's Taxonomy
- UDL-informed design
- explicit standards alignment

These are durable foundations for planning curriculum scope, assessment alignment, and lesson sequencing.

### 2. Human-reviewed AI-assisted development

The research supports a constrained AI-assisted workflow in which:

- AI can be used for drafting and support
- humans remain accountable for release decisions
- sensitive content and assessment work require elevated review

The current evidence does not support autonomous curriculum publishing without human review.

### 3. Accessibility as a baseline requirement

The research supports treating accessibility as a design requirement from the start, not a post-production patch.

For this repository, that means:

- UDL-informed design expectations
- WCAG 2.2-informed digital delivery
- accessible documents, media, and review practices

### 4. Privacy-preserving and low-risk workflow design

The research supports a default stance that the repository should:

- avoid storing student PII
- avoid recommending tools casually
- require vetting for classroom-facing or student-data-adjacent tools

### 5. Review-driven quality control

The research supports building the project around explicit review gates rather than assuming quality emerges from generation alone.

That includes:

- HQIM-informed review dimensions
- lesson and unit rubrics
- accessibility review
- AI human review

## What the Research Supports with Moderate Confidence

### 1. Interoperability-first content design

The research strongly suggests that portable, structured, text-first content with standards tagging and export planning is preferable to platform-specific or PDF-only approaches.

The repo has a good planning basis here, but not yet a full implementation specification.

### 2. Broader claims about open curriculum advantage

The research supports the view that transparency, adaptability, and reduced lock-in are meaningful advantages in curriculum ecosystems.

However, the current repository does not yet have enough comparative empirical evidence to make strong universal claims about:

- cost savings percentages
- superiority across all contexts
- adoption outcomes at scale

Those claims require more case evidence.

## What the Research Does Not Yet Establish Strongly Enough

### 1. Full subject-by-subject curriculum production basis

The current research defines strong general principles, but it does not yet provide deep enough content-specific research across major subjects and grade bands to serve as the sole basis for broad curriculum generation.

### 2. Strong empirical implementation claims

The current case-study layer is still early. It provides useful exemplars, not yet robust implementation evidence.

### 3. Final long-term governance answers

The repository now has strong baseline governance documents, but future implementation will still need:

- more detailed contributor workflows
- release procedures
- maintenance ownership patterns
- subject-specific review roles

## Practical Conclusion

The repository is now ready to support:

- next-stage curriculum architecture work
- contributor workflow design
- pilot artifact generation under review controls
- standards-tagging and packaging planning

The repository is not yet ready to claim:

- that the research base is complete
- that all major strategic claims are fully evidenced
- that large-scale curriculum buildout should proceed without additional subject-specific research

## Recommended Next Research Priorities

1. Expand subject-specific research for math, literacy, science, and civics.
2. Add stronger case studies with adoption and implementation evidence.
3. Strengthen synthesis documents so every major claim is explicitly sourced and confidence-rated.
4. Add more references for culturally responsive pedagogy, competency-based learning, localization, and offline delivery.

## Confidence

High confidence in this assessment.

Reason:

- the current research base is materially stronger and more usable than before
- the remaining gaps are now clear and concrete rather than structural or ambiguous
